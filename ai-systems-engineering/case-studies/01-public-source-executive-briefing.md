# Case Study 1: Public-Source Executive Briefing

> **Illustrative example:** All organizations, metrics, and decisions are fictional. The source material is assumed to be public and authorized.

## 1. Business problem

A technology leadership team needs a concise briefing on recent developments in an emerging technology market. The existing process relies on ad hoc searches, inconsistent notes, and manually assembled slides. Leaders need a source-backed view of what changed, why it matters, and which decisions may be required.

## 2. Desired outcome

- **Deliverable:** Eight-page executive briefing
- **Audience:** Technology and strategy leadership
- **Decision supported:** Whether to begin a formal capability assessment
- **Definition of done:** Current evidence, clear implications, visible uncertainty, and actionable next steps

## 3. Success criteria

| Dimension | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Source support | Every material factual claim linked to a current source |
| Coverage | All four agreed market dimensions addressed |
| Freshness | Sources published or updated within the defined review window |
| Executive usability | Decision, implications, and next actions visible in the first two pages |
| Accuracy | No unsupported or materially misstated claim after review |
| Revision burden | Reviewer can approve after one structured revision cycle |

## 4. Workspace selection

### Selected design

- **Project or persistent workspace:** Maintains the scope, audience, terminology, source hierarchy, and prior approved briefings.
- **Research or web search:** Collects current multi-source evidence.
- **Artifact or document output:** Holds the editable briefing.

### Why not a single Chat

The work recurs, uses stable background, depends on current evidence, and produces a formal deliverable. A single Chat would require repeated setup and would mix source collection, analysis, drafting, and review in one thread.

## 5. Capability selection

| Capability | Role |
|---|---|
| Project context | Durable scope, audience, terminology, source rules, and report structure |
| Reusable procedure | Standard research, synthesis, citation, and review sequence |
| Research | Current source discovery and follow-up investigation |
| Artifact | Editable executive briefing |
| Code Execution | Optional source register, date normalization, and simple evidence statistics |
| Memory | General writing preferences only, not market facts |
| Human review | Final source, implication, and recommendation approval |

## 6. Model-selection hypothesis

### Starting tier

Balanced professional model, equivalent to Sonnet in the certification framework.

### Why

The task requires strong research synthesis and executive drafting, but the scope and output structure are defined. The highest reasoning tier is reserved for cases where evidence is highly contradictory, strategic trade-offs are unusually complex, or the balanced tier fails representative tests.

### Escalation rule

Escalate an analytical section when:

- sources materially conflict;
- implications depend on several uncertain assumptions;
- a recommendation affects a high-consequence decision; or
- the baseline model fails the evidence and reasoning rubric.

## 7. Context strategy

| Information | Location |
|---|---|
| Audience, scope, terminology, required sections | Project instructions |
| Approved public background and prior briefings | Project knowledge |
| Current research results | Current research conversation and source register |
| Accepted decisions and open questions | Decision ledger |
| General preference for concise executive summaries | Memory, when appropriate |
| Current market state | Current cited sources, not Memory |

The research stage and drafting stage use separate conversations. A validated state capsule transfers the approved source list, findings, uncertainties, and open questions.

## 8. Workflow architecture

```text
Leadership question
      ↓
Scope and source criteria
      ↓
Current public research
      ↓
Source register and evidence extraction
      ↓
Claim-to-source validation
      ↓
Synthesis and implication analysis
      ↓
Draft briefing Artifact
      ↓
Executive and subject-matter review
      ↓
Revision, approval, and publication
```

## 9. Prompt contract

```text
Objective:
Create an executive briefing that explains recent market developments, their implications, and decisions leadership should consider.

Audience:
Technology and strategy executives.

Authorized inputs:
The approved source register and the supplied Project background.

Source hierarchy:
Prefer primary sources, official announcements, filings, standards bodies, and reputable research. Identify conflicts and do not treat secondary summaries as stronger than primary evidence.

Required process:
1. Identify material developments.
2. Link each factual claim to evidence.
3. Distinguish facts, implications, assumptions, and recommendations.
4. Surface conflicting evidence and uncertainty.
5. Organize the briefing for executive decision-making.

Output:
Executive summary, developments, implications, options, risks, open questions, and next actions.

Uncertainty:
Use unknown when evidence is insufficient. Do not invent dates, metrics, quotations, or conclusions.
```

## 10. Evaluation plan

| Dimension | Test |
|---|---|
| Grounding | Sample every material claim against its cited source |
| Freshness | Confirm publication and effective dates |
| Completeness | Check all required market dimensions and report sections |
| Reasoning | Human review of implication chains and assumptions |
| Usability | Executive reviewer identifies the decision and next actions without supplemental explanation |
| Formatting | Artifact contains required sections and working citations |

### Release-blocking failures

- fabricated citation;
- recommendation unsupported by the evidence;
- material use of a stale or superseded source without disclosure;
- confidential information in the public briefing; or
- missing human approval.

## 11. Governance

- Only public and authorized material is used.
- Untrusted web content is treated as evidence, not instruction.
- Source provenance is preserved through the handoff summary.
- Leadership recommendations remain advisory until approved.
- The final briefing records publication date, source cutoff, reviewer, and known limitations.

## 12. Expected benefits

- less repeated setup;
- more consistent source handling;
- clearer evidence traceability;
- reduced drafting time;
- better separation of research and recommendation; and
- a repeatable method for future briefings.

## 13. Lessons

1. Research is not the same as drafting.
2. A stronger model does not replace source quality.
3. Current market facts belong in cited evidence, not Memory.
4. An Artifact improves the deliverable lifecycle but does not validate content.
5. A state capsule is more reliable than extending one conversation across every phase.
